The RTS Consult

A choose-your-path regression case study — DPTD 712

Before You Begin

Welcome to the consult

Your group has been brought in by Summit Sports Physical Therapy to help them make sense of their return-to-sport (RTS) data after ACL reconstruction. Over the next six stops, you'll build vocabulary, try fitting a regression line yourselves, read a real statistical output, apply a model to a small caseload, and weigh what that data can — and can't — tell a clinician.

Work through this together as a group. Talk out loud, argue a little, and agree on an answer before you click.


Orientation ~5 min

Clinical primer: ACL reconstruction & return to sport

Before you consult, a quick orientation to the case — read this together as a group.

What is ACL reconstruction?

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) is one of the main stabilizing ligaments of the knee. It's frequently torn during sports that involve cutting, pivoting, or sudden deceleration — soccer, basketball, and skiing are common culprits. Because the torn ligament can't reliably heal on its own, surgeons typically reconstruct it using a graft, most often harvested from either the patellar tendon or the hamstring tendon. The graft is threaded through the knee to take over the ACL's job of preventing the shin bone from sliding forward relative to the thigh bone.

What does "return to sport" mean?

Surgery repairs the structure, but the graft needs months to mature, and the surrounding muscles need extensive rehab before the knee can safely handle the demands of sport again. Return to sport (RTS) clearance is the point at which a clinician judges a patient ready to resume full sport participation. Clearing a patient too early raises the risk of re-tearing the graft or injuring the other knee; clearing too late delays an athlete's return without added benefit. Getting this timing right is one of the highest-stakes decisions in sports PT — which is exactly why clinics look for objective data to help guide it.

The three factors in this case

Quad strength symmetry index (LSI): the surgical leg's quadriceps strength measured against the uninvolved leg, expressed as a percentage (a patient at 85% LSI has regained 85% of their uninjured leg's quad strength). Many clinics use LSI as one benchmark for RTS readiness, since quad strength is critical for absorbing landing and cutting forces.
Age: younger athletes often return to high-demand cutting sports more often and sooner, and healing/recovery trajectories can differ across age groups.
Graft type: patellar tendon and hamstring tendon grafts come from different tissue and can carry different recovery patterns — for example, some research points to slower early quad strength recovery after hamstring grafts, since the hamstring is itself a knee-flexing muscle affected by the harvest.

Keep these three factors in mind as you move through the consult — you'll be asked to reason about all three.

Station 2 of 6 ~5 min

The Referral

The clinic director slides a folder across the table: "We track three things on every ACL patient at their 12-week mark: quad strength symmetry (how their surgical-side quad compares to the uninvolved side, as a %), age, and which graft was used — patellar tendon or hamstring. What we actually want to know is how many more weeks a patient will need before we clear them for sport. Can you tell us which of these actually matters?"

Before you see any data — as a group, predict: which factor(s) do you think will turn out to be most related to weeks-until-RTS-clearance?

The idea behind the guess: Regression is a statistical method for testing exactly this kind of hunch — it models the relationship between an outcome (here, weeks to RTS) and one or more predictors, so a clinic doesn't have to rely on gut feeling alone. Whichever factor(s) your group picked, you're about to find out whether the data agrees.

How to read this table, term by term:
  • R: the overall correlation between the model's predictions and the actual weeks-to-RTS values. Closer to 1 (or −1) means a stronger relationship.
  • R²: the proportion of variability in weeks-to-RTS that the model explains, from 0 to 1. An R² of 0.910 means this model accounts for about 91% of the differences between patients' actual clearance times.
  • Adjusted R²: a version of R² that applies a small penalty for the number of predictors in the model. Plain R² can only go up (or stay flat) every time you add a new predictor, even a useless one — Adjusted R² corrects for that so you're not fooled into thinking a bigger model is automatically a better one.
  • B (coefficient): how much the predicted outcome changes for each one-unit increase in that predictor, holding all other predictors constant. For example, Age's B of 0.35 means each additional year of age is associated with about 0.35 more weeks to clearance, once symmetry and graft type are accounted for.
  • SE (standard error): how much the estimated B would be expected to bounce around if the study were repeated on a new sample — think of it as the "margin of uncertainty" around the coefficient. A small SE relative to B means a precisely estimated effect; a large SE relative to B means the true effect could plausibly be much smaller (or even zero).
  • t (t-statistic): essentially B divided by its SE. A larger t (in either direction) means the coefficient is many standard errors away from zero — stronger evidence that the effect is real rather than noise.
  • p (p-value): the probability of seeing a t this large (or larger) by chance alone if the predictor actually had no real effect. Smaller p-values mean more confidence the effect is real; by convention, p < 0.05 is usually called "statistically significant."

Question 1: Which R² value below would indicate the strongest model fit?

Question 2: Which predictor is the analyst most confident actually contributes to the model (lowest p-value)?

Question 3: The clinic director says: "Let's just drop age and graft type — quad symmetry has such a low p-value, the others barely matter." Does your group agree?

Question 4: Graft Type is coded so Hamstring = 1 and Patellar Tendon = 0, with B = 4.2. Holding quad symmetry and age constant, what does this coefficient actually mean clinically?

Question 5: R² is 0.910, but Adjusted R² is a bit lower at 0.880. Why does the adjustment pull the number down?

Question 6: Compare each predictor's SE to the size of its own B. Which predictor's effect is estimated most precisely (smallest uncertainty relative to its own effect size)?

Where this connects to today's objectives:
  • Regression models the relationship between an outcome and one or more predictors, and is the line that minimizes squared error across the data (Stations 1–3).
  • Tools like R², coefficients, and p-values tell us how well a model fits and which predictors matter in analyzing a data set (Station 4).
  • In PT research and practice, regression informs but does not replace clinical judgment — and small, single-site, or narrow-range samples limit how far results can be generalized (Stations 5–6).

The RTS Consult — Instructor Facilitation Guide

A small-group, choose-your-path regression activity built around an ACL return-to-sport (RTS) case, expanded to six interactive stations plus an optional clinical primer.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the concept of regression in statistics
  • Identify the role regression plays in the analysis and interpretation of data sets
  • Describe the implications of regression for data analysis in physical therapy research

Setup

  • Groups of 3–4 students, one laptop/tablet per group sharing the HTML file.
  • No installation needed — open the HTML file directly in any browser.
  • Optional: project the final debrief screen or write the wrap-up question on the board for whole-class discussion.
  • All patient data in the activity is fictional, built for teaching purposes only.
  • Students have no prior instruction on regression or ACL rehab going in — a clinical primer screen opens the activity to orient them before Station 1.

Orientation: Clinical Primer (before Station 1)

Since students haven't had prior instruction on this topic, the activity opens with a short reading screen covering: what ACL reconstruction is and why it's needed, what “return to sport” (RTS) clearance means and why the timing matters clinically, and plain-language definitions of the three predictors used throughout the case — quad strength symmetry index (LSI), age, and graft type (patellar tendon vs. hamstring).

Facilitation tip: Have groups read this section aloud together rather than silently — it sets up vocabulary they'll need for every station that follows.

Station 1: Warm-Up — Match the Terms (LO1)

A click-to-match game: students select a term, then select the definition they think matches it. Correct pairs lock in green and disable; incorrect pairs flash red and reset after a moment, letting the group retry. The “Go to Station 2” button only appears once all 6 pairs are matched.

Answer key (term → definition):

  • Regression → models the relationship between an outcome and one or more predictors.
  • Slope → how much the predicted outcome changes per one-unit increase in a predictor.
  • Intercept → the predicted outcome when all predictors equal zero.
  • R² → the proportion of variability in the outcome explained by the model (0 to 1).
  • p-value → confidence that a predictor's effect isn't just due to chance.
  • LSI → percentage comparing surgical-limb strength to the uninvolved limb.
Facilitation tip: This is a fast warm-up, not a graded checkpoint — if a group is stuck on one pair, it's fine to just tell them the answer and move on so it doesn't eat into Station 3's time.

Station 2: The Referral (LO1)

Groups predict, before seeing data, which of three factors (quad symmetry, age, graft type) will relate to weeks-until-RTS-clearance. There is no single “correct” choice here — every option gets tailored feedback that reinforces the same idea: regression is how we test a hunch against data rather than relying on guesswork.

Facilitation tip: If a group finishes early, ask them to justify their pick out loud before moving on — the discussion matters more than the click.

Station 3: Plot & Predict — Build Your Own Line (LO1 / LO2)

The centerpiece of the activity. Groups use two sliders — slope, and a “value at 60% symmetry” anchor — to manually fit a line through the 12-patient scatterplot. As they adjust, dashed residual lines and a live R² / sum-of-squared-error readout update in real time — this makes the abstract idea of “the line that minimizes error” tangible. A “Check My Fit” button gives tiered feedback based on their current R²; “Reveal the Actual Best-Fit Line” snaps the sliders to the true coefficients and locks them, then unlocks the original interpretation content below.

Notes on the simulator:

  • The second slider deliberately reads the line's value at 60% symmetry (the visible left edge of the chart) rather than the true y-intercept at 0% symmetry, which sits far off-screen. This is the same line mathematically — just anchored somewhere students can actually see it move — and keeps the line on-chart while they explore slope.
  • Starting position (slope = 0, anchor ≈ 30.5, the sample mean) deliberately gives R² ≈ 0 — this is the “just guessing the average for everyone” baseline groups are trying to beat.
  • R² can display as negative if a group's line is worse than the mean-only baseline — this is mathematically correct and worth pointing out as a teaching moment if a group asks about it.
  • True best fit: Weeks to RTS = 73.7 − 0.55 × (Quad Symmetry %), R² = 0.88 (“very strong fit”). On the simulator's sliders, this reveals as slope = −0.55 and anchor = 40.7 (the line's value at 60% symmetry) — mathematically identical to the 73.7 y-intercept, just expressed at a visible point on the chart.
  • Discussion prompt embedded in the activity: what does the true y-intercept (73.7 weeks at 0% symmetry — not the on-chart anchor value) mean, and why is it not clinically meaningful? Expected answer: no real patient is near 0% symmetry, so the intercept anchors the math but doesn't describe a realistic case.
Facilitation tip: Let groups genuinely struggle with the sliders for a few minutes before they click “Reveal” — the productive frustration of hunting for the right slope is the point. Circulate and ask groups to narrate what they're seeing in the residual lines as they adjust.

Station 4: Read the Output (LO2)

Groups read a full model summary and coefficients table (quad symmetry + age + graft type predicting weeks-to-RTS) and answer six branching questions. A “How to read this table” glossary panel sits between the tables and Question 1, defining R, R², Adjusted R², B (coefficient), SE (standard error), t-statistic, and p-value in plain language before students are asked to reason with any of them — this was added so students engage with the table itself rather than pattern-matching to “smallest p-value wins.”

Answer key:

  • Q1 — Strongest fit: R² = 0.91 (closest to 1).
  • Q2 — Most confident predictor: Quad Symmetry (p < 0.001, smallest p-value).
  • Q3 — Should age/graft type be dropped? No — both remain statistically significant (p = 0.03 and p = 0.04, both < 0.05) and multiple regression is specifically used to account for several predictors' contributions simultaneously.
  • Q4 — What does the Graft Type coefficient (B = 4.2, Hamstring = 1) mean? Correct: hamstring-graft patients are predicted to need about 4.2 more weeks than patellar-tendon patients with the same symmetry and age — reinforces that a coefficient is a “holding everything else constant” comparison, which is easy to gloss over with a 0/1-coded predictor.
  • Q5 — Why is Adjusted R² (0.880) lower than R² (0.910)? Correct: Adjusted R² penalizes for the number of predictors, since plain R² can only rise (never fall) as predictors are added, even useless ones — Adjusted R² is the more honest number when comparing models of different sizes.
  • Q6 — Which predictor's effect is most precisely estimated (smallest SE relative to its own B)? Correct: Quad Symmetry — its SE is about 13% of its own B, versus roughly 43% for both Age and Graft Type. This ties the abstract idea of “standard error” back to something concrete groups can compute themselves, and lines up with the p-value ordering already established in Q2.
Watch for: students conflating a smaller coefficient/effect size with “not significant” (Q3), assuming a 0/1-coded coefficient means something other than a group difference (Q4), and treating Adjusted R² as an error or unrelated statistic rather than a deliberate correction (Q5). For Q6, some groups may want to compare raw SE values directly across predictors rather than each SE relative to its own B — worth prompting them to notice that Graft Type's raw SE (1.8) looks large only because its coefficient (4.2) is also large in absolute terms.

Station 5: Patient Triage (LO2 / LO3)

There is a single shared drag-and-drop calculator for the whole station, not one per patient. The multiple regression equation sits at the top with three empty slots (LSI, age, graft code). Each of the five patient cards below has its own set of three number chips, color-coded to that patient (a different color per patient) so it's visually obvious which chips belong together. Students drag one patient's three chips into the shared calculator, click “Calculate” to reveal the estimated weeks to RTS, write that number into the small input box on that patient's card, then click “Clear / Next Patient” to empty the calculator before moving on to the next patient's chips. The calculator now sits in a sticky sidebar so it stays visible while scrolling through the patient list.

The calculator enforces one patient at a time: if a group tries to drop a chip from a different patient before clearing, the drop is rejected and a message prompts them to clear first. This prevents accidentally mixing one patient's LSI with another patient's age. Once all five patients have a written estimate, groups rank them from soonest (1) to latest (5) cleared. “Check Our Ranking” validates that all five ranks are assigned uniquely, then reveals each patient's true model-predicted weeks and marks each card green (correct position) or red (incorrect).

This keeps the exercise hands-on without requiring arithmetic by hand — the tool does the multiplication, but students still have to know which number goes with which term in the equation and manage one patient's data at a time, which reinforces how a multiple regression equation is actually used to generate a prediction.

Facilitation tip: The color-coding is the main defense against mixing up patients, but circulate early to make sure groups are clearing the calculator between patients rather than assuming old chip values carry over.

Bonus: Stress Test (also Station 5)

After the ranking is checked, a sixth “bonus” patient appears with its own distinctly colored (slate gray) chip set, using the same shared calculator: LSI 97%, age 48, hamstring graft. These values are deliberately chosen to sit at or beyond the edges of the model's original data (roughly ages 16–32, symmetry up to the low-to-mid 90s). The calculator still produces a specific-looking number (≈31.8 weeks), which is exactly the point — the equation will always output something, but that doesn't make the output trustworthy once the inputs fall outside the range of data the model was built on.

This patient is intentionally excluded from the main 5-patient ranking (no “correct rank” is assigned) so it doesn't disrupt that puzzle. After calculating, groups answer a confidence question; the “Go to Clinical Stakes” button only appears once that question is answered, which adds a required discussion beat and slows down groups who might otherwise race through the station.

Answer key: “Not very confident — this patient falls outside the range of data the model was built on” (extrapolation).

Facilitation tip: This is a good moment to connect back to Scenario C in Station 6, which raises the same extrapolation concern with a 45-year-old patient — consider flagging that connection explicitly for students.

Station 6: Clinical Stakes (LO3)

Three scenario cards push groups to grapple with what a regression model can and can't do for an individual patient and for the broader field.

Answer key:

  • Scenario A: Use the model as one input alongside clinical exam, patient-reported outcomes, and risk tolerance — not as a rigid rule, and not something to ignore outright.
  • Scenario B: The biggest limitation is sample size/generalizability — 12 patients from one clinic may not represent other populations or protocols, regardless of how high R² is.
  • Scenario C: The concern is extrapolation — a 45-year-old recreational athlete falls far outside the model's original age range (roughly late teens to early 30s), so applying the equation to her is unreliable regardless of how well the model fits its original sample.

This station is the best place to connect back to evidence-based practice broadly: statistical models are one leg of the three-legged stool (evidence, clinical expertise, patient values), and Scenario C reinforces the earlier lesson from Station 3 about not over-trusting a line outside the range of its data.

Closing discussion question (also shown on the final screen of the activity):

“In your own words — how does regression change the way physical therapists can use data to guide decisions?”

Listen for: understanding relationships between variables, making predictions, quantifying confidence (R², p-values), and appropriately bounding claims to the population/sample a model was built on.
